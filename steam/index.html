How to build a steam engine

I've done it. I've bottled lightning: I've come up with something I call the steam engine. It took a few nights of tossing and turning, but after a while I decided it was only right to let the world know. The idea is pretty revolutionary: you boil water to move things and generate useful work—

Eh? What's that? That's Watt's? A couple guys named Thomas beat me to the punch?

Well, I guess I'll have to come up with my own steam engine then: at least I was a few hundred years late, so the name's in the public domain. Here's my brand new idea: how about I boil water to move things and generate useful work?

It won't be boring, useless work like spinning a magnet to generate electricity. No, I'm going to use this pot of boiling water to do things like predict the weather and recognize numbers...things usually reserved for machines with a screen and a keyboard. That's right: I'm going to turn a pot of boiling water into a literal reservoir computer. But because I'm poor and live in California, where water must never be used frivolously, I'm going to do all of this virtually...for now.

How to simulate a steam engine

Now we're talking. As it turns out, simulating a pot of boiling water is actually a highly nontrivial computational task, enough that a single bubble can get you a publication. The reason is the fact that bubbles can take all sorts of twisty, complicated shapes. While they follow the same set of laws as good old liquid water, they're made of steam, a material with a totally different density, viscosity, and pressure than water.

Solving the fluid equations means keeping track of the tangent vectors to bubbles, which can split apart and combine willy-nilly and take on all sorts of ungodly shapes (see above). My solution? Rather than assuming a spherical cow, I'm going to assume square bubbles. That's right—I'm going to approximate my pot of boiling water as a set of voxels, each of which are either steam or liquid at a given moment.

To be sure, this is about as good as a sketch of a sketch, but it's chaotic, and that's what really matters for a reservoir computer. Let's start!

The first thing to note is that I haven't really made my life too much easier. This is still a multiphysics simulation: I need to simulate the heat transfer from the burners to the water via the pot, the change in phase from gas to liquid, the movement of liquid water, and the interaction between gaseous and liquid water. For the first two effects I'll just use Laplace heat transfer; for the rest, I'll have to discretize the Navier-Stokes equations. When the temperature of a voxel exceeds some threshold, I will say it has turned to gas.

We represent the flow velocity as the scalar field \(\mathbf{u}\) and the density as \(\rho\). Now, in fact, assuming we have a system where all the water stays in the pot, we have the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations: $$ \begin{align} \rho\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\right) &= \rho\mathbf{F}-\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} \end{align} $$ where \(p\) is the pressure and \(\mu\) the viscosity. \(\mathbf F\) is the external field vector—in our case we're just going to assume that every part of the water feels a gravitational force pointing downwards that is proportional to the local density, i.e. \(\mathbf{F} = -\rho\mathbf{e}_z\) everywhere: $$ \begin{align} \rho\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\right) &= -\rho^2\mathbf{e}_z-\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} \end{align} $$ Now, as a prime example of saved effort: even though I am integrating over the entirety of the pot, when I render this I will literally just apply the velocity field to the top layer of voxels, since that's all we see and therefore care about. This will be fine as long as I keep things at a rolling boil.

We now need to couple these equations to the heat input from the burners, which are governed by the Poisson equation. The coupling will enter via the \(p\) and \(\mu\) terms. The hot water both advects and diffuses throughout the pot, so we need to use the field \(\mathbf{u}\) in the incompressible advection-diffusion equation $$ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \nabla\cdot(D\nabla T - \mathbf{u}T) + \mathbf{s} $$ where \(T\) is the temperature of the liquid, \(D\) the diffusivity, and \(\mathbf{s}\) a time-varying scalar field source term for input heat (aka our burners). More on the exact form of \(\mathbf{s}\) later.

All that remains is to relate the temperature of the water to its pressure \(p\) and viscosity \(\mu\), which is to say its phase. Continuing my caveman approximation, I say that above some threshold \(T_\text{boil}\), the phase of the water is steam; otherwise, it is liquid. For now, let's call the boiling pressure \(p_b\) and the liquid pressure \(p_l\); likewise define \(\mu_b,\mu_l\). Then I define the sigmoidal transition from liquid to boiling: $$ p = p_l + S(T-T_\text{boil})(p_b - p_l) \qquad \mu = \mu_l + S(T-T_\text{boil})(\mu_b - \mu_l) $$ where the sigmoid function is \(S(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\alpha x}}\) for some scale \(\alpha\).In other words, it's as I said: every voxel is either steam or gas, as determined by temperature.

So, our final set of coupled equations is: $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} &= -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}-\rho\mathbf{e}_z-\frac{\nabla p}{\rho} + \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} \\ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} &= \nabla\cdot(D\nabla T - \mathbf{u}T) + \mathbf{s} \end{align} $$ i.e. the Navier-Stokes and advection-diffusion equations. (Note the kinematic viscosity \(\nu = \mu/\rho\).)

We also have the state equations $$ \begin{align} p &= p_l + S(T-T_\text{boil})(p_b - p_l) \\ \mu &= \mu_l + S(T-T_\text{boil})(\mu_b - \mu_l) \end{align} $$ for the pressure and viscosity.

I've neglected to consider the interfacial temperature conditions and thermodynamic equations of state entirely here, so this simulation is more qualitative than anything, but it will look pretty boily and, again, be chaotic enough for our purposes. This is only because we are considering "macroscale" boiling.

As always, it takes five minutes to write the PDEs and five years to solve them. However, since I've chosen a voxel-based discretization, things are not so bad here.
