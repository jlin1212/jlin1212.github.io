How to simulate a steam engine

Now we're talking. As it turns out, simulating a pot of boiling water is actually a highly nontrivial computational task, enough that a single bubble can get you a publication. The reason is the fact that bubbles can take all sorts of twisty, complicated shapes. While they follow the same set of laws as good old liquid water, they're made of steam, a material with a totally different density, viscosity, and pressure than water.

Solving the fluid equations means keeping track of the tangent vectors to bubbles, which can split apart and combine willy-nilly and take on all sorts of ungodly shapes (see above). My solution? I'm going to cop out of the problem entirely. To be sure, this is about as good as a sketch of a sketch, but it's chaotic, and that's what really matters for a reservoir computer. Let's start!

As much as I would like to fully solve the two-phase model and reproduce cavitation—the formation of bubbles—I'm trying to make a demo that will run in the browser. Cavitation problems are hilariously expensive and we'd be here for several days just waiting for a bubble to form. So instead, I'm going to use what are known as stochastic Navier-Stokes models: the chaos comes from random noise rather than numerical precision. This is going to save me a lot of integration time, and also be much more stable.

How to simulate a water engine

We start with the incompressible Navier-Stokes flow equations. Now, wait, you might say, isn't steam one of the most compressible things around? You'd be right to say so, but the compressible flow equations are also quite expensive to simulate. Instead, what we will mostly model here is the transport of pressure waves in a liquid, so technically the pre-boiling convective waves. First, the deterministic incompressible flow equations are $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} &= \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \frac{\nabla p}{\rho} + \frac{\mathbf{F}}{\rho}\qquad \\ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} &= 0 \end{align} $$ which are the momentum and continuity equations respectively. Normally the external force field is just gravity, \(\mathbf{g}\), but I'm going to input heat-induced expansion in this term at the bottom of the pot. Finally, we assume constant kinematic density.

Now we add our noise term. The most straightforward method is to just straight-up add noise to the momentum equation, so that all previous assumptions we end up with $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} &= \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \nabla p + \mathbf{F} + \mathbf{\xi}\qquad \\ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} &= 0 \end{align} $$ with our Gaussian white noise vector field \(\mathbf{\xi}\).

As a final constraint, taking the divergence of the momentum equation and using the incompressibility condition allows us to derive the commonly-used constraint \(\nabla^2 p = -\nabla\cdot((\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u})\). Now, in the simplest finite difference approximation of the time derivative we have \begin{align} \mathbf{u}^{(n+1)} &= \mathbf{u}^n + h\left(\nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}^n -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n - \nabla p^n + \mathbf{F}^n + \mathbf{\xi}\right)\qquad \\ 0 &= -\nabla^2 p^n -\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n \end{align} If we take \(\mathbf{u}^0(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}\), then \(p^0(\mathbf{x}) = 0\), and the resulting sequence is found iteratively.

To make it easy to solve these equations implicitly, I'm going to use a meshless solving method. Let our domain be \(\Omega\). In my case I want to do the solutions in 3D Fourier space: \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) &= \int_\Omega \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{x} \\ \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) &= \int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k} \end{align} I represent the Fourier transform of \(\mathbf{u}\) as \(\overline{\mathbf{u}}\). We likewise have \(\overline{\mathbf{F}}\) and \(\overline{p}\). Since the Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian, we just keep \(\xi\) as-is.

The following identities hold for derivative operators in Fourier space: \begin{align} \overline{\nabla\mathbf{u}} &= i\mathbf{k}\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}} \\ \overline{\nabla^2\mathbf{u}} &= \overline{\nabla\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}} = i\mathbf{k}\cdot(i\mathbf{k}\otimes\mathbf{u}) = -(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{k})\overline{\mathbf{u}} = -|\mathbf{k}|^2\mathbf{u} \\ \overline{\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}} &= i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{u}} \\ \end{align} Here \(\otimes\) denotes the outer product. These will be useful in what comes next...

I want to show the derivation of the convective term in 3D Fourier space, because I think it's pretty cool. We have \begin{align} (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} &= \left(\left[\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1) e^{i\mathbf{k_1}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\right]\cdot\nabla\right)\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2) e^{i\mathbf{k}_2\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_2 \\ &= \left(\left[\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1) e^{i\mathbf{k}_1\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\right]\right)\cdot\int_\Omega i(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k_2})) e^{i\mathbf{k}_2\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k} \\ &= \iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right]e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2)\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2 \end{align} Now, taking the Fourier transform of the result yields \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) = \iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right]\left(\int_\Omega e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})\cdot\mathbf{x}}\,d\mathbf{x}\right) \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2 \end{align} It can be shown that \(\int_\Omega e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})}\,dV \propto \delta(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})\). The above integrand becomes: \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) = \int_\Omega\left(\iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right] \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2\right)\,d\mathbf{k} \end{align} All terms of the integrand now satisfy \(\mathbf{k}_2 = \mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}_1\), so we can write the single integral \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) &= \int_\Omega i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot((\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1)\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\right] \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \\ &= \int_\Omega i(\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1) \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \\ &= \int_\Omega (\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot i(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1) \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \end{align} where I used the contraction identity \(\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\otimes\mathbf{c}) = (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\mathbf{c}\). The result is pretty interesting: it's not often that we see an equation where you transform into Fourier space with the goal of convolving in there (rather than multiplying).

Since this term is extremely important, I will briefly describe what computing it entails:

  1. First, of course, we compute the Fourier transform \(\overline{\mathbf{u}}\).
  2. Next, to compute the gradient, we take the \(\mathbf{k}\)-field, which should have the same shape as \(\mathbf{u}\), and take the outer product \(\mathbf{ik}_{ij} \otimes \overline{\mathbf{u}}_{ij}\). In einsum index notation this is the operation ...i,...j->...ij.
  3. Finally, we wish to convolve the vector field \(\overline{\mathbf{u}}\) with the matrix field \(\overline{\nabla\mathbf{u}}\). One way to do so is to first compute ...i,...kmn->ki to get every possible pair of matrix-vector products. Then, compute ...ij->j to contract over all of them.

While this would obviously work, it's insanely expensive. The list above is probably better-interpreted as a way to get intuition for what the integral actually means. In practice, given \(\overline{\mathbf{u}}, \overline{\nabla\mathbf{u}}\), it's actually cheaper to transform back into coordinate space and just directly compute \((\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\), then transform back into Fourier space. Each FFT only costs \(\mathcal{O}(N\log N)\), so this makes a lot more sense! In Python, for a simulation in a \(L \times L \times L\) domain, this would be something like

u_bar = np.fft.fftn(u_real, axes=[0,1,2]) # (L, L, L, 3) kspace = np.fft.fftfreq(L) # (L,) kmesh = np.meshgrid(*[kspace]*3) # (L, L, L) x 3 kvecs = np.stack(kmesh, axis=3) # (L, L, L, 3) nabla_u_bar = 1j * np.einsum('...i,...j->...ij', u_bar, kvecs) # (L, L, L, 3, 3) nabla_u_real = np.fft.ifftn(nabla_u_bar, axes=[0,1,2]) # (L, L, L, 3, 3) convect_real = np.einsum('...i,...ij->...j', u_real, nabla_u_real) # (L, L, L, 3) convect_bar = np.fft.fftn(convect_real, axes=[0,1,2]) # (L, L, L, 3)

In Fourier space, then, our system is described by \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}^{(n+1)} &= \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + h\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}-i\mathbf{k}\overline{p}^n+\overline{\mathbf{F}}^n+\xi\right) \\ 0 &= i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n \end{align} Why did I omit the pressure constraint? Now that I've done this transform, we can solve for it directly: $$ \overline{p}^n = -\frac{(i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n})}{|\mathbf{k}|^2} $$ Might as well sub it in, right? Now we just have a single equation: \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}^{(n+1)} &= \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + h\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n}-i\mathbf{k}\frac{(i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n})}{|\mathbf{k}|^2}+\ldots+\xi\right) \\ &= \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + h\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n}+\frac{\mathbf{k}(\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}})}{|\mathbf{k}|^2}+\ldots+\xi\right) \\ &= \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + h\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\underbrace{\left[\mathbf{I}-\frac{\mathbf{k}\otimes\mathbf{k}}{|\mathbf{k}|^2}\right]}_\text{$\mathbb{P}$}\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n}+\ldots+\xi\right) \end{align} Notice the appearance of the famous Leray projector, \(\mathbb{P}\). Wait a minute, what happened to our external force field \(\overline{\mathbf{F}}^n\)? It turns out that customarily part of the input force is absorbed into the pressure in order to make it divergence free, something we can also do using \(\mathbb{P}\): \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}^{(n+1)} = \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + h\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\left[\mathbf{I}-\frac{\mathbf{k}\otimes\mathbf{k}}{|\mathbf{k}|^2}\right]\left(\overline{(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n}+\overline{\mathbf{F}}^n\right)+\xi\right) \end{align} One more potential problem! What happens when \(\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{0}\)? In this case we take \(\mathbb{P}=\mathbf{0}\). We're now ready to simulate the system fully in Fourier space. To get its "actual" state at any time, we can just do an inverse Fourier transform!

What it looks like to simulate a water engine

My ultimate goal is a full 3D simulation, but let's start in two dimensions first to keep things simple. In this case we have the up-down vector \(\mathbf{e}_y\) and the left-right vector \(\mathbf{e}_x\), which are standard basis vectors.

We want to compute a mapping from \([0,I_\max]^\ell \to [O_\min,O_\max]^m\), where \(I_\max\) is the maximum burner output intensity and \(O_\min,O_\max\) are the minimum and maximum water offsets at the pot surface.

The only thing I need to specify is my force field. I'll assume that wherever I have a burner, there exists a Gaussian force pointing upwards. Let the locations of the burners be \(\mathbf{b}_0,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_p\). The equation for the external force is then \begin{equation} \mathbf{F}^n(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=0}^{\ell-1}\mathbf{e}_y\mathbf{s}^n_i e^{-|(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}_i)|^2/\sigma^2} \end{equation} with some constant \(\sigma\) roughly proportional to the burner radius. \(\mathbf{s}^n_i\) represents the intensity of the \(i\)-th burner at timestep \(n\) and is controlled externally.

To start, I want to input time-varying sine waves at evenly-spaced inputs. In this case \(\mathbf{s}^n_i = \sin(an)\sin(an+bi)\) for some constants \(a, b\), and \(\mathbf{b}_i = (L\frac{i-1}{\ell+1}, 0)\). The only other variable I have to define is \(\mathbf{u}^0 = \mathbf{0}\), and I'm set to simulate. I'm going to be running NumPy directly in the browser using Pyodide.

Let's see what we get. I'm running at a very low-resolution \(H=W=64\) grid because anything larger would take about 10 seconds per frame.
