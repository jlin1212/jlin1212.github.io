How to build a steam engine

I've done it. I've bottled lightning: I've come up with something I call the steam engine. It took a few nights of tossing and turning, but after a while I decided it was only right to let the world know. The idea is pretty revolutionary: you boil water to move things and generate useful work—

Eh? What's that? That's Watt's? A couple guys named Thomas beat me to the punch?

Well, I guess I'll have to come up with my own steam engine then: at least I was a few hundred years late, so the name's in the public domain. Here's my brand new idea: how about I boil water to move things and generate useful work?

It won't be boring, useless work like spinning a magnet to generate electricity. No, I'm going to use this pot of boiling water to do things like predict the weather and recognize numbers...things usually reserved for machines with a screen and a keyboard. That's right: I'm going to turn a pot of boiling water into a literal reservoir computer. But because I'm poor and live in California, where water must never be used frivolously, I'm going to do all of this virtually...for now.

How to simulate a steam engine

Now we're talking. As it turns out, simulating a pot of boiling water is actually a highly nontrivial computational task, enough that a single bubble can get you a publication. The reason is the fact that bubbles can take all sorts of twisty, complicated shapes. While they follow the same set of laws as good old liquid water, they're made of steam, a material with a totally different density, viscosity, and pressure than water.

Solving the fluid equations means keeping track of the tangent vectors to bubbles, which can split apart and combine willy-nilly and take on all sorts of ungodly shapes (see above). My solution? I'm going to cop out of the problem entirely. To be sure, this is about as good as a sketch of a sketch, but it's chaotic, and that's what really matters for a reservoir computer. Let's start!

As much as I would like to fully solve the two-phase model and reproduce cavitation—the formation of bubbles—I'm trying to make a demo that will run in the browser. Cavitation problems are hilariously expensive and we'd be here for several days just waiting for a bubble to form. So instead, I'm going to use what are known as stochastic Navier-Stokes models: the chaos comes from random noise rather than numerical precision. This is going to save me a lot of integration time, and also be much more stable.

We start with the incompressible Navier-Stokes flow equations. Now, wait, you might say, isn't steam one of the most compressible things around? You'd be right to say so, but the compressible flow equations are also quite expensive to simulate. Instead, what we will mostly model here is the transport of pressure waves in a liquid, so technically the pre-boiling convective waves. First, the deterministic incompressible flow equations are $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} &= \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \frac{\nabla p}{\rho} + \frac{\mathbf{F}}{\rho}\qquad \\ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} &= 0 \end{align} $$ which are the momentum and continuity equations respectively. Normally the external force field is just gravity, \(\mathbf{g}\), but I'm going to input heat-induced expansion in this term at the bottom of the pot. In addition, we have the no-slip boundary condition that \(\mathbf{u} = 0\) on all boundaries of the pot but the very top. Finally, we assume constant kinematic density.

Now we add our noise term. The most straightforward method is to just straight-up add noise to the momentum equation, so that all previous assumptions we end up with $$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial\mathbf{u}}{\partial t} &= \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \nabla p + \mathbf{F} + \mathbf{\xi}\qquad \\ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} &= 0 \end{align} $$ with our Gaussian white noise vector field \(\mathbf{\xi}\).

As a final constraint, taking the divergence of the momentum equation allows us to derive the commonly-used constraint \(\nabla^2 p = -\nabla\cdot((\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u})\). Now, in the simplest finite difference approximation of the time derivative we have \begin{align} \mathbf{u}^{(n+1)} &= \mathbf{u}^n + h\left(\nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}^n -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n - \nabla p^n + \mathbf{F}^n + \mathbf{\xi}\right)\qquad \\ 0 &= \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}^n \\ 0 &= -\nabla^2 p^n -\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{u}^n\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}^n \end{align} If we take \(\mathbf{u}^0(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}\), then \(p^0(\mathbf{x}) = 0\), and the resulting sequence is found iteratively.

To make it easy to solve these equations implicitly, I'm going to use a meshless solving method. Let our domain be \(\Omega\). In my case I want to do the solutions in 3D Fourier space: \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) &= \int_\Omega \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{x} \\ \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) &= \int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k} \end{align} I represent the Fourier transform of \(\mathbf{u}\) as \(\overline{\mathbf{u}}\) (blame my E&M professor). We likewise have \(\overline{\mathbf{F}}\) and \(\overline{p}\). Since the Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian, we just keep \(\xi\) as-is.

The following identities hold for derivative operators in Fourier space: \begin{align} \overline{\nabla\mathbf{u}} &= -i\mathbf{k}\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}} \\ \overline{\nabla^2\mathbf{u}} &= \overline{\nabla\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}} = -i\mathbf{k}\cdot(-i\mathbf{k}\otimes\mathbf{u}) = -(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{k})\overline{\mathbf{u}} = -i|\mathbf{k}|^2\mathbf{u} \\ \overline{\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}} &= -i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{u}} \\ \end{align} Here \(\otimes\) denotes the outer product. These will be useful in what comes next...

I want to show the derivation of the convective term in 3D Fourier space, because I think it's pretty cool. We have \begin{align} (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} &= \left(\left[\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1) e^{i\mathbf{k_1}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\right]\cdot\nabla\right)\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2) e^{i\mathbf{k}_2\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_2 \\ &= \left(\left[\int_\Omega \overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1) e^{i\mathbf{k}_1\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\right]\right)\cdot\int_\Omega i(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k_2})) e^{i\mathbf{k}_2\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k} \\ &= \iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right]e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2)\cdot\mathbf{x}} \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2 \end{align} Now, taking the Fourier transform of the result yields \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) = \iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right]\left(\int_\Omega e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})\cdot\mathbf{x}}\,d\mathbf{x}\right) \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2 \end{align} It can be shown that \(\int_\Omega e^{i(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})}\,dV \propto \delta(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2-\mathbf{k})\). The above integrand becomes: \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) = \int_\Omega\left(\iint_{\Omega \times \Omega} i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}_2\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_2))\right] \,d\mathbf{k}_1\,d\mathbf{k}_2\right)\,d\mathbf{k} \end{align} All terms of the integrand now satisfy \(\mathbf{k}_2 = \mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}_1\), so we can write the single integral \begin{align} \overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}) &= \int_\Omega i\left[\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot((\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1)\otimes\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\right] \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \\ &= \int_\Omega i(\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1) \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \\ &= \int_\Omega (\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}_1)\cdot i(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1))\overline{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}_1) \,d\mathbf{k}_1 \end{align} where I used the contraction identity \(\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\otimes\mathbf{c}) = (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\mathbf{c}\). The result is pretty interesting: it's not often that we see an equation where you transform into Fourier space with the goal of convolving in there (rather than multiplying).

In Fourier space, then, our system is described by \begin{align} \overline{\mathbf{u}}^{(n+1)} &= \overline{\mathbf{u}}^n + ih\left(-\nu|\mathbf{k}|^2\overline{\mathbf{u}}^n-\overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}}+i\mathbf{k}\overline{p}^n+\overline{\mathbf{F}}^n+\xi\right) \\ 0 &= i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{\mathbf{u}} \end{align} (Unfortunately I'm not doing anything here with \(i\hbar\), as cool as that would be.) Why did I omit the pressure constraint? Now that I've done this transform, we can solve for it directly: $$ \overline{p}^n = -\frac{(i\mathbf{k}\cdot\overline{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}})}{|\mathbf{k}|^2} $$ We're now ready to simulate the system fully in Fourier space. To get its "actual" state at any time, we can just do an inverse Fourier transform!
